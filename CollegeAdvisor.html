Before we build anything
How would you decide which schools to apply to?
If you were Alex — 3.4 GPA, CS major, 30 hours of work a week — how would you figure out which schools actually want a student like you? Let's look at the options.
🌐 Look at college websites and rankings Wrong information ▶
College websites tell you what they want in an ideal student. Rankings tell you prestige. Neither tells you whether you specifically — your GPA, your major, your work hours — have a realistic shot. The intersection between what Alex wants and what the college will actually accept is invisible from the outside.
🗣️ Ask friends who transferred Too thin ▶
Your friend got into UC Santa Cruz with a 3.6 GPA in CS. Does that mean Alex will too, with a 3.4 and 30 work hours a week? Maybe. But that's one data point. What you need is the pattern across hundreds of students with profiles like Alex's. No individual friend can give you that.
🧑‍💼 Ask a transfer counselor — the best answer, but... Doesn't scale ▶
This is the closest thing to a right answer. A counselor with ten years of experience has watched hundreds of students apply and seen what happened. They know intuitively which schools will bite and which won't — not because the college website says so, but because they've seen the pattern repeat. But there is one counselor for every 500 students. Their knowledge is private, lives in their head, and isn't available at midnight when Alex is filling out applications.
So what do we do?
The counselor is the right answer — but they can't reach everyone. They've synthesized thousands of outcomes into an intuition. The question is: how do we give College Advisor that same knowledge?
We can't give a machine years of experience. But we can give it the data that experience was built on — thousands of real transfer outcomes from California community colleges. The machine finds the same pattern the counselor found. That's machine learning.

Step 4 Evaluate — Is the Model Any Good?

We hold back some students from training and test the model on them. But which metric matters most depends on the cost of being wrong.

Accuracy
80%
8 of 10 test students predicted correctly. Sounds good — but is it?
False Negative Rate
30%
30% of students who would have been admitted were told "no" — and may never apply. This is the dangerous error.
Precision
85%
When we say "admitted," we're right 85% of the time.
Recall
70%
We only catch 70% of students who could have transferred. 30% are missed opportunities.
💬 Notice that Precision and Recall are evaluation metrics — they measure the model after it's built. They are not part of building it. This is a key distinction in ML: building the model vs. evaluating the model are two separate steps.
Limitation of Stage 1 — what motivates Stage 2
Alex got a "YES" — but can she afford it? Classification tells Alex which schools will likely accept her. It says nothing about financial aid. Getting in and being able to attend are two different questions. Alex now asks: "How much aid will I likely receive?" — and that requires predicting a number, not a yes/no. That is a regression problem.
