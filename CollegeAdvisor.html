What would you do if you were Alex?
Before we build a solution, let's understand what Alex would actually try — and why each approach falls short.
🌐 Look at college websites and rankings Wrong information ▶
College websites tell you what they want in an ideal student. Rankings tell you prestige. Neither tells you whether you specifically — your GPA, your major, your work hours — have a realistic shot. The intersection between what Alex wants and what the college will actually accept is completely invisible from the outside.
🗣️ Ask friends who transferred Too thin ▶
Your friend got into UC Santa Cruz with a 3.6 GPA in CS. Does that mean Alex will too, with a 3.4 and 30 work hours a week? Maybe. But that's one data point. What you need is the pattern across hundreds of students with profiles like Alex's. No individual friend can give you that.
🧑‍💼 Ask a transfer counselor — the best answer, but... Doesn't scale ▶
This is the closest thing to a right answer. A counselor with ten years of experience has watched hundreds of students apply and seen what happened. They know intuitively which schools will bite for a profile like Alex's. But there is one counselor for every 500 students. Their knowledge is private, lives in their head, and isn't available at midnight when Alex is filling out applications.
So what do we do?
The counselor is the right answer — but they can't reach everyone. They've synthesized thousands of outcomes into an intuition. The question is: how do we give every student access to that same knowledge?
We can't give a machine years of experience. But we can give it the data that experience was built on — thousands of real transfer outcomes. The machine finds the same pattern the counselor found. That's machine learning.

💡 Both systems take the same input and produce the same output. The difference is what sits in the middle. In traditional software, a human studies the data, finds the patterns, and writes the rules. In ML, the machine does that — faster, at larger scale, and it can update itself when the data changes.
But the brain needs to be trained first
Before the ML system can make predictions, it needs to learn from historical data. This is called training. Here's the full pipeline:
The brain is built once during training — then used instantly for every new prediction. The machine is not deployed until it passes evaluation in Step 3.
💡 This pipeline is the same regardless of which ML algorithm we use — decision tree, logistic regression, clustering, or recommendation. What changes at Step 2 is which algorithm we train and how it finds patterns. That's what each stage of College Advisor teaches.

Week 5 Preview Why Decision Trees don't always work ▶

The decision tree draws a hard line — one point below the threshold and you fall off a cliff. Logistic Regression replaces that sharp wall with a smooth probability curve.

💡 Alex has a 3.4 GPA. The decision tree says NO. Logistic Regression says 58% — right in the grey zone. A 58% probability means: apply, but also apply to safer schools. The decision tree gave false certainty. Logistic regression shows the grey.
